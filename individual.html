Individual protection

Why We Are Designing Extraterrestrial Base Safety Systems Fundamentally Wrong

There is a persistent image in any conversation about space safety: an astronaut in a spacesuit, ready for an EVA. It is cinematic, and it is almost entirely irrelevant to what daily life on an extraterrestrial base actually looks like.

The uncomfortable truth is this: the most dangerous place on a Martian base or lunar outpost will not be the surface. It will be the corridor.

Where People Actually Spend Their Time

Consider what a functional extraterrestrial base requires. Mining operations, processing facilities, storage, workshops, a medical bay, greenhouses, server rooms, living quarters, kitchens. All of it connected by electrical cables, pipes, and ducts running through pressurized spaces, linking the places people think of less often — pump rooms, filtration and recirculation stations, battery compartments, compressor rooms and tanks, hangars, warehouses, and the most fantastical combinations of all of the above. Once a base becomes a serious operational facility — not a four-person research station, but a real working installation — it begins to look less like a space station and more like an underground industrial complex.

That means hundreds of meters of corridors. Branching tunnels. Large hangar spaces filled with equipment. Production areas. Inter-module passages. People will spend 95% of their time in these spaces — repairing equipment, moving materials, holding meetings, cooking food, simply living.

The spacesuit, meanwhile, is not being worn — thankfully. It hangs in a locker. Or stands in the airlock. Or is in the next compartment — a hundred meters down the corridor. Or it is supposed to be there, but was taken away for cleaning and tank replacement.

The Physics of a Problem That Is Routinely Ignored

Depressurization is not what the movies show. In films there is time for a heroic sprint, for dramatic decisions, for a final word. In reality, physics works differently.

At 0.5 atmospheres — a perfectly reasonable operating pressure for reducing structural loads on the base — the pressure differential during depressurization is lower than on the ISS. This slows the process somewhat. But "somewhat" here means the difference between "instantaneous" and "ten seconds to critical pressure drop, plus another ten to fifteen before loss of consciousness."

Twenty seconds. That is all you have in a pessimistic but realistic scenario. It begins with a draft that stirs your hair. A couple of seconds in which you must react.

In twenty seconds, an average person under stress is capable of: recognizing what is happening — 3–5 seconds. Deciding to act — another 2–3 seconds. Beginning to move toward the spacesuit. Running to it, if it is in the same room. Ah, wait. In the planned settlements, gravity is no higher than 0.38g. Running is difficult, though there is a chance that the airflow — filled with crates, cables, robots, and wrenches — will pick the colonist up and carry them to the suit locker.

But that is unlikely. They will not make it. Under any circumstances.

This is not a question of fitness or composure. It is a question of physics and geometry. A standard spacesuit takes several minutes to don even for an experienced person. Fast emergency suits take thirty seconds at minimum. Neither fits within a twenty-second window.

What Current Safety Doctrine Proposes — and Where It Falls Short

It would be dishonest to say no one has thought about this. They have. The ISS has a practiced evacuation to the spacecraft — the crew knows the routes, distances are minimal, everything is close at hand. For Artemis lunar bases, "safe havens" — pressurized refuges reachable during an emergency — are being seriously discussed. But as already noted, moving through a lunar station against an oncoming airflow is extremely difficult.

The real problem is different: all of these solutions are designed for a small crew in a confined space. They do not scale.

When corridors grow to hundreds of meters, when people work in dozens of different compartments simultaneously — "run to the refuge" stops being a plan and becomes a lottery. Not because the engineers did poor work. Simply because the problem was framed for different conditions.

It is precisely this class of protection — something between "nothing" and "full spacesuit," for a person in a corridor, in a workshop, in a kitchen — that is the least developed of all.

Framing the Right Question

The problem is being framed incorrectly. The question is not "how do we make a spacesuit that goes on faster." The question is: how do we provide a person with basic protection against depressurization at any moment, without any preparatory action?

The answer is obvious once the question is framed correctly: the protective device must be on the person at all times. Not in a locker. Not in the airlock. On the person.

This immediately raises the next question: what exactly needs to be on the person to give them the critical minutes in a depressurization event?

Not the full protection of a spacesuit. Not the ability to work on the surface. Simply: sealing of the head and airways, basic body compression against barotrauma, a few minutes of oxygen, communications and navigation. That is enough to reach a spacesuit, a refuge, or to wait for help.

Concept: Everyday Clothing as the First Line of Defense

Imagine a jacket. An ordinary-looking work jacket — lightweight or insulated depending on the compartment. It is worn continuously, like any garment. No discomfort, no bulk.

Folded flat in the collar is a hood made of effectively airtight fabric. At rest, it is invisible. In an emergency — one motion deploys it into a capsule around the head.

The seal is not created by silicone gaskets — they are too stiff to fasten in a panic. The zippers are ordinary, light, closeable with one hand in a few seconds. Airtightness is achieved differently: the zipper is coated with microcapsules containing two components. When zipped, the capsules rupture, the components mix, and a chain reaction begins. The substance turns into foam within five seconds, filling every micro-gap. Single-use — but for an emergency situation, that is exactly what is needed.

A built-in cartridge delivers oxygen. It also slightly inflates the hood, creating buffer pressure — protecting the head from flying wrenches — and fills compression chambers in the jacket, guarding against barotrauma during a rapid pressure drop. Five to ten minutes of oxygen. That is enough.

In the face section of the hood is a flexible transparent OLED display. When off, it is simply transparent. When activated, it shows a map, the location of the leak, the route to the spacesuit or refuge, and data from built-in cameras. Communication via integrated speakers and microphone. If there is no power — just a transparent panel in front of the face. Still better than nothing.

The trousers and footwear of the same system provide compression for the legs. Because an inflated hood around the head with a depressurized body is half a measure. Compression is needed everywhere. Of course, with this approach, perfect airtightness cannot be achieved. But it does not need to be.

Why This Is Not Being Done

The honest answer: because we are not yet building real bases. As long as space facilities are small stations with minimal crews, where a spacesuit is genuinely always nearby, the problem does not feel critical. The ISS is a confined volume where any emergency equipment is seconds away.

But the moment we move to bases with hundreds of meters of corridors, with dozens of people working in different compartments simultaneously — all safety logic requires rethinking.

The spacesuit remains essential equipment for work and extended surface operations. But — and we will return to this — working outside in a spacesuit will be the exception, not the rule.
